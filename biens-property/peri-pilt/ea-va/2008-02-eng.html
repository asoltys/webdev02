A word from the Director

As we enter the final quarter of the fiscal year, the Valuation and Payments in Lieu of Taxes Programs Directorate (VPILT) can look back on some important achievements and forward to exciting new initiatives. Over the past few months, we have received most of the applications for payments in lieu of taxes from taxing authorities across the country. Our staff is working diligently to process these payments in a fair and equitable manner according to our mandate under the Payments in Lieu of Taxes Act. The thumbnail sketches of the PILT Program in each region presented below gives some idea of the scope of this work.

The Valuation side of the Directorate has worked equally hard to deliver its services to clients and stakeholders. The Valuation Program has responded to many requests for appraisal services in support of real estate transactions. One recent project was a comprehensive study on market values that facilitated the sale of seven major federal assets. The team has also been busy redeveloping its innovative accounting tool, the Book Value Calculator (BVC), to tailor it to the needs of Canadian municipalities that are required to have accrual accounting in place by 2010.

The BVC is a computer modelling application for estimating the book value of real property assets. It was originally developed to help the federal government move to an accrual accounting regime in 2001. Since then the BVC has been in high demand, both in Canada and abroad, by other organizations seeking to modernize their accounting processes. Clients have included the Province of Ontario's realty corporation and the province's school boards. Now the Association of Municipal Managers, Clerks and Treasurers of Ontario wishes to use the BVC for all municipalities in the province. This will be a large undertaking for VPILT, requiring the modification of the tool to accommodate different kinds of municipal assets and infrastructures as well as the training of users. The success of the project in Ontario may lead to requests for the BVC from municipalities across Canada.

Initiatives such as the BVC help widen the scope of our services. Through other initiatives we play a leadership role in the valuation community. For example, we are developing "Green" appraisal guidelines in partnership with Canada's two professional appraisal organizations. (See Valuation Advisor, January 2007, "Green valuation practices to be developed.")

We are very proud of the hard work and dedication of our VPILT Team across the country. Our aim, as always, is to go the extra mile.

Colin Boutin
Director
Valuation and Payments in Lieu of Taxes Programs

Environmental Scan

The following environmental scan provides broad forecasts of anticipated changes in federal payments in lieu of tax (PILT) amounts for 2008-2009 and a summary of factors that may impact on PILT and property taxes next year across Canada. These synopses are based on the best information currently available and are intended as general guidance for our federal client organizations.

National Overview

For the nation as a whole, PILT amounts are expected to rise by approximately 2 to 3% next year. Additions to inventory, new construction and renovations will raise the PILT beyond the general estimated increases presented in the regional summaries.

British Columbia

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - BC Assessment (publicly funded provincial Crown Corporation governed by a Board of Directors)
  • Relevant legislation - Assessment Act of British Columbia
  • Taxation authorities - incorporated municipalities, provincial government and some First Nations
  • For more information: Government of British Columbia and British Columbia Property Assessment

Yukon

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Yukon Territorial Government
  • Relevant legislation - Yukon Territorial Assessment and Taxation Act
  • Taxation authorities - Yukon Territorial Government, incorporated municipalities
  • For more information: Government of Yukon and Yukon Property Assessment
Forecast

Due to probable tax-rate adjustments, economic growth and new development, PILT and property tax amounts are likely to increase by 3 to 9%, in addition to any increases caused by inventory changes.

Other developments
  • The strong growth of the high technology, knowledge economy, international trade and resource sectors and the boom associated with the 2010 Olympics are putting great pressure on Vancouver's office market, primarily in the downtown core.
  • The total value of the province's 2007 assessment roll was approximately $808 billion, a 23% increase over 2006.
  • 1,783,780 properties were valued for the 2007 assessment roll, an increase of 1.9% compared to 2006.
  • In the Yukon, any significant PILT changes will likely be inventory-related. First Nations claims settlements leading to transfers of federal property to First Nations jurisdiction continue to reduce the number of federal properties subject to PILT.

Alberta

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Local assessors are responsible for all property assessments, except in the case of linear property, which is the responsibility of the Province of Alberta.
  • Taxing authorities - Municipal governments
  • Relevant legislation - Municipal Government Act
  • For more information: Alberta - Municipal Affairs Assessment Services
Forecast

We expect PILT amounts to rise by about 5% for taxation year 2008, mainly due to Alberta's annual inflation rate. With assessments increasing due to higher market values, municipalities are using a "revenue neutral" calculation to adjust the previous year's tax rate.

Other developments
  • A summary report is now available on ASSET, Alberta's Internet-based, assessment-data-sharing program. The report summarizes property assessments by their property tax liability code. The PILT Program is investigating whether it will be possible to use ASSET as a tool for PILT purposes.
  • The Equalized Assessment Panel (EAP), a provincially appointed technical committee, recently recommended that equalized assessments be based on each municipality's taxable assessment for the current year. The measure is designed to ensure consistency in determining municipal and school taxes. Alberta's municipal affairs ministry predicts that this will result in a higher degree of accuracy of provincial assessments and greater confidence in the system. The earliest this change is expected to take place is 2008. For further information on the EAP, please refer to the following Web site: Alberta - Municipal Affairs

Saskatchewan

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Saskatchewan Assessment Management Authority, as well as individual municipal assessors for the Cities of Regina, Saskatoon, North Battleford, Moose Jaw, Swift Current and Prince Albert
  • Taxing authorities - Municipal governments
  • Relevant legislation - The Urban Municipality Act, 1984; The Rural Municipality Act, 1989; The Municipalities Act (a consolidation of the former Northern Municipalities and Cities acts)
  • For more information: Government of Saskatchewan
Forecast

We expect PILT amounts to increase by approximately 3% for the 2008 taxation year.

Other developments
  • The Saskatchewan government has continued with the education rebate credit for the 2008 taxation year. The government increased the rebate from 8 to 10% on residential and commercial properties, while the farmland tax credit remained the same.
  • Saskatchewan is preparing for a general reassessment in 2009, one that will use a more market-based approach to set property values.

Manitoba

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Provincial Municipal Assessor or the City of Winnipeg
  • Taxing authorities - Municipal governments
  • Relevant legislation - The Municipal Assessment Act; The Local Government Districts Act; The City of Winnipeg Act; and The Northern Affairs Act
  • For more information: Manitoba - Property Assessment
Forecast

We expect PILT amounts to increase by approximately 5% in most municipalities for the 2008 taxation year.

Other developments
  • All properties across Manitoba were reassessed in 2006, based on 2003 market values. In accordance with the Municipal Assessment Act, properties must be reassessed every four years. The 2009 taxation year will conclude the current four-year assessment cycle.
  • The application of appropriate "market modifiers" to federal property values continues to require much consultation between assessment and PILT officials.

Northwest Territories

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - The Government of the Northwest Territories is responsible for assessment services in all of the territory with the exception of the City of Yellowknife, which provides its own valuation service. The NWT government has the option of contracting assessment services from private companies.
  • Relevant legislation - The Property Assessment and Taxation Act
  • Taxing authorities - Municipal governments and the territorial government
  • Taxation areas - Municipal Taxation Area (Yellowknife, Hay River, Fort Smith, Inuvik, Fort Simpson, Norman Wells) and the General Taxation Area (hamlets, settlements and Unorganized Area)
  • For more information: Northwest Territories - Municipal and Community Affairs
Forecast

We expect PILT amounts to increase on average by 2 to 3% for taxation year 2008.

Other developments
  • The City of Yellowknife is implementing a new assessment roll for 2007. On average, federal values have increased by 20 to 25%, however, the mill rate will be adjusted to off-set most of the increases.
  • On August 8, 2007, Prime Minister Stephen Harper announced the expansion of Nahanni National Park in the NWT. This initiative will continue to be monitored with Parks Canada officials to determine an effective date.

Nunavut

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Qikiqtaaluk Corporation
  • Taxation authorities - Town of Iqaluit and Nunavut Territorial Government
  • Relevant legislation - NWT Property Assessment and Taxation Act
  • For more information: Government of Nunavut
Forecast

We expect minimal PILT increases in taxation year 2008.

Other developments

The assessment cycle in Nunavut is every 10 years, and the last general reassessment was completed in 2005. Over the next few years, the only changes to assessment values will occur where there have been physical changes to a property made through renovation, demolition, or additions. Mill rates in Nunavut have not changed from 2006, and no change is foreseen for 2008.

Ontario

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Municipal Property Assessment Corporation
  • Taxing authority - Municipal governments, school boards in unorganized areas
  • Relevant legislation - Ontario Assessment Act, Ontario Municipal Act
  • For more information: Government of Ontario
Forecast

We tentatively predict an overall PILT increase of approximately 1 to 2% for the 2008 taxation year due to municipal tax-rate increases. An accurate forecast is difficult to make because of the complexity of Ontario's tax legislation. The Budget Measures Act, 2004, gives municipalities various options for calculating property taxes on commercial, industrial and multi-residential properties. These options include tax caps of 5% or 10%, or caps of 5% on current-value assessments and claw-backs of tax decreases.

Other developments
  • After receiving many complaints about its assessment process and after the release of a critical report by the Ontario Ombudsman in March 2006, the Province of Ontario decided to freeze the 2006 assessed values until the end of taxation year 2008. The Government of Ontario is proposing the following three key changes to improve the fairness and predictability of assessments;
    1. four-year reassessment cycles (in lieu of annual reassessments);
    2. the phase-in of assessment increases on residential properties;
    3. improvements/enhancements to the fairness and effectiveness of the appeal system:
      • a mandatory request for reconsideration as the first stage of the appeal process;
      • an appeal deadline that follows the completion of the reconsideration process,
      • the establishment of information disclosure requirements at both stages (request for reconsideration and appeal).
  • Ontario's Budget Measures Act has changed the rules for determining tax levels on new construction. Municipalities no longer have to base their calculations on the average tax level of six comparable properties. Under the legislation, tax calculations on new construction will be based on 90% of the assessed value in 2007 and on 100% in 2008 and beyond.
  • To enhance the province's investment climate, the Government of Ontario plans to cut the Business Education Tax (BET) by $540 million over the next seven years. Currently, BET rates across the province vary widely. The target will be a maximum education tax rate of 1.6%, which is well below the current average rate of 1.85%.

Quebec

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Municipalities and regional governments
  • Taxing authority - Municipalities, school boards
  • Relevant legislation - Municipal Taxation Act
  • For more information:Government of Québec
Forecast

We expect residential property values to increase by approximately 30 to 40% and non-residential values by 10 to 20% in 2008. However, we estimate a PILT increase of only 2 to 3%.

Other developments

The Education Act was amended to allow for the averaging of school taxes and to allow taxpayers to pay the school tax in two equal payments when it exceeds the amount set by regulation

Prince Edward Island

  • Assessment authority - Government of P.E.I.
  • Relevant legislation - P.E.I. Real Property Assessment Act (PDF 135.6 KB) (Help with Alternative Formats)
  • Taxation authority - Government of P.E.I.
Forecast

Since P.E.I. is on an annual reassessment cycle and market values and tax rates are stable, the expected increase in PILT amounts will only be about 2%.

Nova Scotia

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Service Nova Scotia and Municipal Relations, Assessment Division
  • Relevant legislation - Nova Scotia Assessment Act
  • Taxation authority - Each individual municipality
  • Relevant legislation - Municipal Government Act
  • For more information: Nova Scotia - Property Assessment and Nova Scotia - Legislature
Forecast

We can expect incremental increases in assessed values across the province. Market-value changes point to minor increases in regions outside the Halifax Regional Municipality (HRM) and moderate increases within the HRM. Changes within Nova Scotia's assessment authority and assessment methodology (see below) may also affect the PILT in 2008.

Other developments
  • Nova Scotia's assessment authority is undergoing two important changes:
    1. It is being transformed from a line department to a municipally controlled not-for-profit corporation called the Property Valuation Services Corporation. As of April 1, 2008, the assessment authority will cease to be a provincial department, although the province will continue to be responsible for assessment policy and legislation.
    2. The assessment authority has purchased a new computer-assisted mass appraisal system, called iasWorld. The new system is based on the Marshall Valuation Services calculator method and has many sophisticated features. Its introduction will impact both commercial and residential costed properties, which had been valued using various Boeckh's-based manuals. Everyone involved is working hard to ensure the new system is fully functional in time to produce the 2008 assessment roll.
  • In April 2005, the Province of Nova Scotia enacted legislation to abolish the Business Occupancy Tax (BOT) by 2012. Business occupancy assessments will be phased out from 2006 to 2012 and completely removed from the roll for the 2013 taxation year. Municipalities are expected to raise tax rates to help offset the revenue losses caused by abolishing the BOT.
  • The provincial CAP Program, which caps the assessed values of residential properties owned by non-commercial residents, is continuing. As of 2008, the cap rate will be tied to the CPI. The details of this initiative are included in the changes to the Assessment Act.

New Brunswick

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Service New Brunswick
  • Taxation authority - Finance Department, Province of New Brunswick
  • Relevant legislation - New Brunswick Assessment Act and Regulations
  • For more information: New Brunswick - Property Assessment
Forecast

PILT amounts are expected to increase by about 4% for taxation year 2008, in addition to any increases due to inventory acquisitions and new construction.

Other developments
  • The Province is continuing to convert to the use of Marshall Swift Costing Manuals as its method for property assessment. Instead of implementing the change everywhere, immediately, the Province is phasing in the new system over several years and on a municipal-unit-to-municipal-unit basis.
  • The assessed values of commercial/industrial property across New Brunswick will likely increase by about 4%, while residential values will increase marginally.
  • No legislative or policy changes are expected in 2008 that will affect PILT-eligible federal property.

Newfoundland

Assessment and taxation system
  • Assessment authority - Municipal Assessment Agency and City of St. John's
  • Relevant legislation - Newfoundland Assessment Act and City of St. John's Assessment Act
  • Taxation authority - Each individual municipality
  • For more information: Newfoundland - Property Assessment
Forecast

Newfoundland and Labrador is in the second year of a triennial assessment cycle. As a result, assessed values will remain the same in 2008, except for properties that have undergone physical changes. Tax rates are also expected to be stable throughout the province; therefore we expect PILT increases to be minimal.

Other developments

The government of Newfoundland and Labrador has passed new assessment legislation, which establishes a "special-purpose property" category for assessment purposes. Properties in this category must be valued according to their reproduction cost, plus physical depreciation; economic or functional obsolescence may not be considered in the valuation. According to the Municipal Assessment Agency Inc., examples of special-purpose property include: tank farms, refineries, airports, lighthouses, mines and quarries, smelters, transshipment facilities, marinas,wharves and hangars.

Full steam ahead for Dispute Advisory Panel

Some 30 months into its mandate the Dispute Advisory Panel (DAP) has made significant progress in helping resolve disagreements between taxing authorities and the federal government over PILT issues. Panel members have completed hearings in three complex cases and have delivered decisions on all three to the Minister of Public Works and Government Services. Another five hearings are currently underway. At the same time VPILT regional staff have been seeking common ground with municipalities on a wide variety of other cases and hopefully will resolve a number of issues.

The DAP was appointed in May 2005 as an impartial, independent third party to advise the Minister of PWGSC in resolving disputes involving property value, property dimension, effective rate and payment supplements that arise between PWGSC and the taxing authorities. (The Panel does not hear cases on whether a property is eligible for PILT.) In addition to its chairman, the Panel consists of 34 members who, in total, represent every province and who have been chosen for their expertise in property valuation and/or assessment and taxation law.

The terms of the panel members (excluding that of the chairman which expires in June 2008) will expire in May 2008. The VPILT Directorate is seeking to re-appoint sitting members and to appoint new members to fill any vacancies. Appointments and re-appointments are made by the Governor in Council after consultation with stakeholders such as the Federation of Canadian Municipalities.

The three cases the DAP has heard thus far are:

  1. Rural Municipality of South Cypress
    This southern Manitoba municipality is the home of the Canadian Forces Base Shilo, which has a number of vacant buildings. Under Manitoba assessment practices, vacant properties are eligible for a reduction in assessment value (25% reduction in value for one year of vacancy; 50% reduction for two years). South Cypress requested that the vacancy allowance not be applied to these federal properties. The panel agreed with PWGSC's position.
  2. Rural Municipality of Rockwood
    The federal property at issue in Rockwood (located near Winnipeg) is a group of units inhabited by Correctional Service of Canada inmates. The federal government contends these units should be treated as residential property for tax assessment purposes, while the municipality wants to consider them institutional/commercial. The panel agreed with the municipality's position.
  3. Halifax Citadel
    The municipality argues that the Citadel (the fortifications overlooking the town and harbour) should be assessed without consideration of restrictions of use that apply because of its status as a National Historic Site. The federal government contends these restrictions must be taken into account in determining the market value for tax purposes. The dispute first arose in 1997.

OEAQ conference to be a "green" trailblazer

Conferences are an important part of modern professional life but they can be hard on the environment. Think of the fossil fuels burnt to bring delegates to an event. Consider the energy consumed and waste produced by all those plastic water bottles, Styrofoam cups, reams of paper, and other throw-away items.

The Ordre des évaluateurs agréés du Québec, or OEAQ (Quebec's association of professional appraisers) wants to help reverse the trend by making its October 2008 conference as eco-friendly as possible. Every aspect of the OEAQ conference – location, meals, products, guest speakers – is being planned to have a minimal environmental impact. The 250 to 300 valuation professionals who gather at the Lac Leamy Hilton Hotel in Gatineau, Quebec, next October will have a chance not only to learn about the latest in the valuation field but also to contribute to sustainable development.

The initiative has been spearheaded by an organizing committee of seven OEAQ members, including André Morin, Chief Appraiser of Canada, VPILT, PWGSC. The committee is concerned about the dangers of global warming and is keen to promote green practices within the valuation community. The greening of conferences is increasingly being promoted within government and other organizations, but the concept is still new in the professional and business world. The OEAQ conference organizing committee is working hard to ensure the October 2008 event will be a trailblazer. Some proposed ideas for cutting waste and conserving resources at the conference include:

  • Choice of a central location (Gatineau, Quebec) to minimize travel distances for participants.
  • Rebates on registration fees for delegates who car-pool.
  • Use of tap water, glasses and pitchers instead of bottled water.
  • Meals featuring as much local food as possible (foods trucked from afar entail a high consumption of fuel).
  • Use of china instead of Styrofoam or paper plates and cups.
  • Reducing paper by offering conference presentations on portable memory sticks.
  • Use of recycled paper and other products.
  • Encouraging delegates to reduce, re-use and recycle.
  • Turning off lights and machines when not in use.
  • Inviting guest speakers from Quebec's and Canada's environmental movements.
  • Workshop sessions on green valuation and information on green buildings.

All these measures will reduce the conference's environmental "footprint" and save money too by cutting down on waste and consumption of energy. Delegates will become more aware of what individuals and groups can do to protect our environment. Important too will be the leadership example shown to other organizations.

The organizers are well aware that one eco-friendly conference is just a small step in the battle against global warming. But small steps can lead to more steps and to the proliferation of grassroots action. Such action on many fronts will be needed to keep the earth hospitable for future generations.
